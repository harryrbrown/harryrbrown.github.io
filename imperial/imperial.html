Imperial College: Finances
Where is the money being spent?

Assets and Surpluses


Between 2011 and 2016, Imperial College added a total of £537m to its general reserves, with each year's budget shown below.

During the same time period, the net value of the College's assets rose from £843m to £1,415m - an increase of £572m.

This represents a combined income and increase in asset value of over £1.1bn.

Expenditure


Most of the College's expenditure is covered by its annual income, as seen above. So what is happening with this £1.1bn?

Capital Expenditure

According to this document , the College's operating surpluses are used to fund capital projects. This includes developments such as Woodward Buildings (opened 2015) and the new White City campus (opened 2017).

We can clearly see how capital expenditure has risen substantially in the past few years, with the aforementioned Woodward Buildings and White City campus constructions contributing largely to this.

Add up the numbers, and then take away money provided as part of HEFCE capital grants (£124m between 2011-2016), and we arrive at a total internal expenditure of £539m, or roughly equivalent to the total surplus operated over this time period.

This still leaves virtually all of the asset money untouched - around £570m.

Should the College be doing anything?


The College itself notes in its 2014/15 annual report that "the high cost of living in London can be a deterrent [to students]." Yet it seems the College is doing little to amend this situation. In the Felix report on the Against Acton campaign in 2013, a student-led campaign opposing the construction of a new student halls in Acton, it is noted that "UCAS information shows that the number one reason, by a mile, that students who have been accepted turn down Imperial is living costs."

Four years later and it still appears that Imperial is doing little to make itself a welcoming university for people of all backgrounds. The university charges some of the country's highest rents for its student accommodation; a report from the Independent found Imperial to be the country's most expensive non-private university to attend. For many students applying to Imperial, the cost of the accommodation closest to the main campus - Beit, Eastside and Southside - is almost prohibitively expensive. This leaves such students with a significantly reduced range of accommodation options for their first year at university.

University, and the life that comes with it, should be accessible to anybody who deserves it - not just those who can afford it.

College Accommodation

In 2016/17, the average cost of accommodation at Imperial College was £169.43 per week, or £6,607.77 per year (assuming a 39 week rent). With a total of 2,820 beds available across Imperial's 11 accommodation sites, the College's total income from accommodation was £477,794pw, or £18,633,966 for the academic year.

In real terms, this comes in at around 2% of the College's annual turnover. Is it really so unreasonable to suggest, for the sake of the students who are simply unable to afford living in South Kensington, or are priced out of applying to Imperial altogether, that the College absorbs some of the accommodation costs from its surpluses?

Price Capping

Based on the price structure for accommodation in 2016/17, a cap on all accommodation at £170pw would reduce Imperial's annual income by less than £3m / year, and yet would have a profound impact on the financial situation of many students at the College. Such a move would be a huge step towards encouraging applicants from a wider range of backgrounds to study at the College, and would also make Imperial's accommodation provision much fairer; no longer would South Kensington be accessible only to the university's wealthier students.
